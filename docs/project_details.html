Motivation

Southern California's coastline experiences recurring Harmful Algal Blooms (HABs) driven by the dinoflagellate Lingulodinium polyedra (L. poly). During bloom events, dense concentrations of this bioluminescent organism turn the surf a deep rust-red by day and emit an electric-blue glow at night as waves disturb the water. While visually striking, these blooms signal a serious ecological imbalance: they can deplete dissolved oxygen, threaten marine food webs, and prompt closures of shellfish harvesting and recreational beaches along one of the country's most visited coastlines.

Why it matters: HABs cost the United States an estimated $82 million per year in fisheries losses, public health costs, and tourism damage, and that number is rising as ocean conditions shift.

HABs are becoming more frequent and more intense as anthropogenic climate change alters the ocean conditions that govern them. Warming sea surface temperatures expand the seasonal window in which L. poly thrives. Changes in upwelling patterns and increased agricultural runoff raise nutrient levels that fuel rapid cell division. Meanwhile, ocean acidification from elevated atmospheric CO2 can shift phytoplankton community dynamics in ways that favor bloom-forming species.

Despite this urgency, bloom prediction today still relies heavily on costly, labor-intensive manual water sampling that provides only sparse, delayed observations. Continuous environmental sensors deployed at research piers offer a potentially transformative alternative: they record temperature, pH, salinity, dissolved oxygen, and chlorophyll in real time, every day. Our project asks whether this stream of sensor data, combined with machine learning, contains enough signal to reliably estimate L. poly bloom concentration, without waiting for a lab result. If so, it could form the foundation of a low-cost, near-real-time early warning system for coastal managers, fisheries operators, and the public.

Results

We tested three modeling strategies to predict L. poly concentration and compared how well they captured bloom behavior over time. The main story is simple: baseline regression gives a strong starting point, univariate forecasting captures some timing signal, and multiview modeling gives the most useful forecast skill.

1) Baseline Models

Using IFCB image features only, Random Forest was the strongest baseline model with R² = 0.523 and RMSE = 7.990. Linear Regression and Ridge Regression were lower at R² values of 0.245 and 0.349. This tells us the relationship between morphology and bloom concentration is not purely linear.

2) Univariate Forecasting

When we forecasted from the target history alone, the best effective skill occurred at Tp = 3 days with ρeff = 0.388. Skill dropped in the middle horizons, then showed a smaller return around 20 to 30 days. In plain terms, this model sees some timing structure, but it is not consistently strong across all forecast windows.

Simple takeaway: univariate forecasting can capture near term behavior, but it misses important context from other measurements.

3) Multiview Embedding

Multiview Embedding gave the strongest overall forecast performance. At Tp = 3, all feature sets reached about 0.58 in effective skill, and the combined model had ρeff = 0.581 with RMSE = 0.388. A second strong window appeared near Tp = 25, where the combined model reached ρeff = 0.473. This two peak pattern suggests one short timing cycle and another longer cycle in bloom dynamics.

Methodology

Our workflow started by combining daily environmental sensor readings from Scripps Pier with IFCB image based measurements of L. poly. After aligning everything by date, we cleaned missing values and prepared one daily record that captured both ocean conditions and bloom activity. This gave us a consistent timeline we could use for both modeling and visualization.

We then compared several modeling approaches to understand both accuracy and timing. First, we trained baseline regression models to set a clear reference point. Next, we used time aware dynamic models to forecast bloom behavior across multiple future horizons. In parallel, we built an interactive bubble chart so users can explore how temperature, pH, chlorophyll, and predicted bloom volume change together over time. This combination of models and visualization lets us both measure performance and communicate patterns in a way that is easy to interpret.
